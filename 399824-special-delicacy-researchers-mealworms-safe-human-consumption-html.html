Special delicacy: Researchers say mealworms safe for human consumption

March 31, 2024
Post

- The conclusion of scientists at the EU food safety agency is expected to lead to EU-wide approval within months of yellow mealworm

- This followed an application by the French insect-for-food production company, Agronutris

- The insect’s main components are protein, fat and fibre, offering a potentially sustainable and low carbon-emission source of food for the future

Insects could soon begin crawling their way into dishes on Europe's dinner plates after mealworms were approved as human food.

Mealworms, which are larvae of beetles rather than worms, are already used as an ingredient in pet food.

But the "yuck factor", The Guardian reported, might make it a while before the larvae become a popular item on shopping lists, researchers said.

Mealworms are the first insects approved by the European Food Safety Agency for human consumption (EFSA).

It paves the way for the yellow grubs to be used whole and dried in curries and other recipes and as a flour to make biscuits, pasta and bread.

They are rich in protein, fat and fibre and are likely to be the first of many insects in the coming years to feature on European plates.

Mealworms were the first insect to be assessed by the EU Agency under the 'Novel Food' Regulation, which came into force in 2018 - causing similar applications to flood.

"There is a great interest of the scientific community and also the food industry in the edible insect sector," EFSA food scientist Ermolaos Ververis said.

People are still enjoying tucking into insect bars and cricket burgers in much of the world - including parts of Africa, Australia and New Zealand.

Once the endorsement by the EFSA has been ratified by the European Commission, such food will become available in Europe.

Psychological barriers are, however, believed to be especially high in Europe, which may make it long before the yellow worms start flying off supermarket shelves.

Giovanni Sogari, a social and consumer researcher at the University of Parma in Italy, said:

"There are cognitive factors that make the thought of consuming insects repellent for many Europeans, stemming from our social and cultural experiences - the so-called 'yuck factor.""With time and exposure, such attitudes can change," added the researcher.

Since 2018, EFSA said it has submitted 156 applications for 'novel food' safety assessments covering everything from algae-derived foods to an array of insects.
