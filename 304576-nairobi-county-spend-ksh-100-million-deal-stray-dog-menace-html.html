Nairobi County to spend KSh 100 million to deal with stray dogs menace

April 15, 2024
Post

- County director of veterinary services Murithi Muhari said the county had at least 50, 000 stray dogs

- The World Animal Protection report indicated 73% of city residents were not aware of dog vaccination services indicating most of the canines were dangerous

- The report further revealed 53% of dog owners believe dogs are meant to freely roam around at night without restriction

City Hall has announced plans to spend KSh 100 million taxpayer's money to eliminate stray dogs in Nairobi county.

According to county Director of Veterinary Services Murithi Muhari, the number of stray dogs has increased tremendous and was posing a great risk to city residents.

Muhari who was speaking on Wednesday, May 1, during the launch of a dog survey report by World Animal Protection, said the county had at least 50,000 canines which were roaming in the city and it was high time they were eliminated.

"We are concerned by the increasing number of stray dogs in the city and we are going to eliminate them in the next one year, we have allocated KSh 100 million for that purpose,” he said.

The report indicated 53% of dog owners in the city allow their canines to roam at night with an assumption that they are naturally designated to provide security day and night without restriction.

The report further revealed 73% of residents interviewed said they were not aware of vaccination services for dogs existed, meaning most of the canines were not vaccinated and their bites could cause rabies.

"This survey points to a significant information gap among dog owners, the government and other stakeholders should take it seriously and consider providing education programs to owners so that we minimise the risk to comes with such lapses," said Emily Mudoga, campaign manager at World Animal Protection.

Muriithi said the homeless canines would be 'humanely' killed using a shot gun similar to the one used on cows so that they do not suffer the pain.

The county officer added the dogs were currently being impounded on routine basis and would be killed later.
